Bovara di Trevi, 304 AD

The Tree That
Would Not Die

1,800 years old. Still bearing fruit. This is where our name comes from.

January 28, 304 AD

He Said No.

Emiliano came from Armenia. In 296 AD, Pope Marcellinus consecrated him as the first bishop of Trevi. When Diocletian's persecution reached Umbria, soldiers brought him to Bovara — a pagan holy place — and ordered him to sacrifice to the Roman gods.

He refused. They bound him to a young olive tree and beheaded him. According to a 9th-century codex, wild beasts refused to attack him. Cold froze the executioners' hands. None of it stopped what came next — but the tree they tied him to never died.

Every January 27th, the people of Trevi carry a gilded statue of Emiliano through their medieval streets by torchlight. They have done this for over 600 documented years. Some things do not fade.

Still Alive

Nine Metres.
Three Trunks. One Root.

In Bovara, below the medieval walls of Trevi, stands a tree radiocarbon-dated to 96 BCE. Nine metres of circumference. Three great trunks from a single base. Its heartwood is long gone — hollowed by centuries — but the outer bark is alive and vigorous, dropping olives every autumn without fail.

Every frost that wiped out olive trees across Umbria — this one survived. Every time. The locals call it il Saggio Signore — the Old Sage. It is now a protected monument. You can visit it. You cannot touch it.

The Same Valley

Not a Name.
A Debt.

Our grove sits a few kilometres from that ancient tree. Same valley. Same limestone. The Sant'Emiliano tree carries genetics positioned between the wild Olivastro and the Moraiolo cultivar — the oldest olive DNA in Umbria. Our roots draw from the same ground.

We named this oil after it not as branding — but as acknowledgement. To the tree. To the bishop who refused. To every harvest this land has given since the Roman Empire.
