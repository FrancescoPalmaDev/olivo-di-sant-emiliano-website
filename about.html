A Tree Nearly 2,000 Years Old

The Oldest Witness
in Umbria

In the hamlet of Bovara, just below the medieval hilltop town of Trevi in Umbria, stands one of the most remarkable living monuments in Italy. The Olivo di Sant'Emiliano is an ancient olive tree estimated to be nearly 2,000 years old — a silent, enduring presence that has watched over this valley since the days of the Roman Empire.

Its trunk has a circumference of nine metres, split into three great winding limbs that rise just five metres from the ground. The inner heartwood is long gone, hollowed out by centuries, yet the outer trunks remain alive and vigorous — bearing fruit every autumn just as they have for generation after generation. The locals have always called it the Saggio Signore: the Old Sage.

The tree grows on land that once belonged to the 12th-century Benedictine Abbey of Bovara, whose monks cultivated olive trees as both a practical and spiritual act. It has recently been registered as a protected monument and fenced so visitors can admire it without disturbing the roots — a recognition that some things belong to everyone and to history.

The Legend of the Martyr

Bishop Emiliano,
304 AD

The tree carries its name from one of the most dramatic moments in Umbria's early Christian history. According to a 9th-century codex — one of the oldest written records of the region — Emiliano, the bishop of Trevi, was arrested during the Christian persecutions ordered by the Emperor Diocletian.

In 304 AD, he was brought to this spot, bound to the branches of a young olive tree, and beheaded. The tree, the legend says, was already here to receive him — and it never died. While countless other olive trees across Umbria were lost to the great frosts of subsequent centuries, this one survived every time, always returning with an abundant harvest the following year.

For the people of Trevi, the tree is inseparable from the memory of their patron saint. It is a place of devotion as much as a natural wonder — and a reminder that some roots go too deep to be broken.

From Ancient Roots to Your Table

Why We Named
Our Oil After It

When we set out to make olive oil in this part of Umbria, we kept coming back to the story of that tree. It stands a few kilometres from our grove, in the same valley, drawing from the same limestone-rich soil. It is the oldest and most enduring expression of what this land can produce.

We named our oil Olivo di Sant'Emiliano as an act of respect — for the tree, for the saint, for the Benedictine monks who understood that cultivating an olive grove is itself a form of prayer, and for the generations of Umbrian farmers who kept this tradition alive long enough for us to inherit it.

Every bottle we produce carries a little of that story. We hope you taste it.
